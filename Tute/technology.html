Overview

Over the past five years, massively parallel DNA sequencing platforms have become widely available. As a result, variant data on genomes from healthy subjects and patients are being generated at an unprecedented rate. However, the development of bioinformatics tools for handling this data lags behind, limiting the ability to fully exploit biological content.

Tute Discovery takes your genome variant data in a variety of common formats (VCF, ASM, and more), and gives you results that are evidence-based; that is, for each candidate gene/variant, we will present a human readable annotation with links to external supporting evidence. This allows the most convenient web experience for users to identify disease genes.

To take full advantage of the Tute Discovery platform, users may choose to perform single case, case-control or family studies. Large sample volume is easily accommodated with either the bulk upload feature or multi-sample VCF support. Further parameters of these studies may be applied, including disease model specification (autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, x-linked, and so on), comma-delimited phenotype descriptors, application of custom gene panels to search for specific candidate genes, and a number of filters can also be applied to organize your results. Each analysis is further distilled into a list of candidate variants, genes and pathways, saving you time and effort.

Your results data is always accessible in Tute Discovery, and can be exported to your local hardware at any time.

The Tute Score

Tute Discovery is able to prioritize variant findings based on the use of machine learning. Machine learning is a means by which a system immediately incorporate incoming data into itâ€™s knowledge base in order for the system to learn from that data and tailor its output. Using this technology Tute Discovery analyzes sequencing data and inherently prioritizes harmful genetic variants by what is known as a Tute Score.

The Tute Score is an unprecedented data element that employs a support vector machine (SVM) algorithm to extrapolate the knowledge of over 60 data and annotation sources. The result is a numerical value on a scale of 0-1, which represents likelihood of a variant having deleterious effects. Support Vector Machine (SVM) is one of the most popular supervised classifiers in the field of machine learning and has been widely used in many bioinformatics applications. Tute Discovery optimizes machine learning to identify the optimal hyperplane that creates the largest margin between the training points for neutral and deleterious variants/genes, providing the most precise data prioritization available.
